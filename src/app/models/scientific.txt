
WACP/26/001  Effect of pictorial adherence counselling tools on adherence to antiretroviral therapy in a rural family-practice ART clinic
Authors: Olufemi-Akanle R, Pemi MD, Pemi SA, Okereke NO, Akereke OO, Babalola VO, Gana JN, Yohanna SA
Affiliations: Family Medicine Practice Centre, Gawu-Babangida; Department of Family Medicine, Federal Medical Centre, Bida, Niger State, Nigeria
Correspondence: mdpemi@gmail.com
Background: Adherence to antiretroviral therapy is critical for effective HIV management. Pictorial adherence counselling tools have been suggested to enhance patient understanding and adherence, but evidence in Nigerian antiretroviral therapy clinics remains limited. This study evaluated the effect of pictorial adherence counselling tools on adherence to antiretroviral therapy among clients attending the antiretroviral therapy clinic in Gawu-Babangida.
Objective: To evaluate the effect of pictorial adherence counselling tools on adherence to antiretroviral therapy and treatment outcomes among clients attending the antiretroviral therapy clinic in Gawu-Babangida.
Methods: A quasi-experimental study was conducted among 102 participants, with complete data available for 100 participants, comprising 49 in the control group and 51 in the intervention group. Socio-demographic characteristics, adherence to antiretroviral therapy, knowledge of antiretroviral therapy regimen, reported side effects, and reasons for non-adherence were assessed. The intervention group received monthly pictorial counselling sessions for three months. Adherence, knowledge, and CD4 counts were compared between groups at baseline and week 12 using appropriate statistical tests.
Results: Participants had a mean age of 38.45 ± 7.61 years in the control group and 40.10 ± 10.64 years in the intervention group, with females predominating in both groups (63.3% and 68.6%, respectively). Education level differed significantly between groups (p = 0.035), while other socio-demographic characteristics were comparable. At baseline, adherence, antiretroviral therapy knowledge, and CD4 counts were similar across groups (p > 0.05). By week 12, the intervention group achieved significantly higher adherence, with 64.7% attaining at least 95% adherence compared with 20.4% in the control group (p < 0.001). The intervention group also had higher CD4 counts (504 versus 353; p = 0.010). Knowledge of antiretroviral therapy dose and timing improved in both groups, though differences were not statistically significant (p = 0.701). Common reasons for non-adherence included forgetfulness and travel, while reported side effects were similar between groups, with dizziness being the most frequent. Socio-demographic factors generally did not influence adherence, except religion (p = 0.026).
Conclusion: Pictorial adherence counselling tools significantly improved antiretroviral therapy adherence and CD4 counts among clients, highlighting their potential as a practical strategy to enhance treatment outcomes in antiretroviral therapy clinics.

WACP/26/002  Disclosure and antiretroviral therapy adherence among adolescents living with HIV in Ogun State
Authors: Olugbenga-Bello A, Anegbe N, Ogundipe J, Ilori R
Affiliations: College of Health Sciences, Ladoke Akintola University of Technology, Ogbomoso; Department of Community Medicine, LAUTECH Teaching Hospital; Department of Community Medicine, Federal Medical Centre, Abeokuta
Correspondence: eknorb2@gmail.com
Introduction: Adolescents living with HIV (ALHIV) face psychosocial and behavioural challenges that affect HIV disclosure and adherence to antiretroviral therapy (ART). Despite improved access to ART in Nigeria, adherence gaps remain a concern for optimal treatment outcomes.
Objective: To assess HIV status disclosure and its association with ART adherence among ALHIV attending selected treatment centres in Ogun State, Nigeria.
Methodology: A descriptive cross-sectional mixed-methods study was conducted among 260 ALHIV attending selected ART clinics in Ogun State. Quantitative data were collected using structured questionnaires and analysed in SPSS version 26, while qualitative data from focus group discussions were analysed thematically. Statistical significance was set at p<0.05.
Results: Most participants were female (73.5%), aged 10–13 years (40.4%), and on ART (80.4%). Only 34.2% had disclosed their HIV status, while 69.2% demonstrated good ART adherence. The need for support was the main facilitator of disclosure, whereas fear of stigmatisation was the leading barrier. ART adherence was facilitated by support, motivation, and understanding, but hindered by stigma. Disclosure status was significantly associated with age, sex, school level, and duration since HIV diagnosis (p<0.05). Adherence was significantly associated with age, sex, school level, living arrangement, and duration since diagnosis (p<0.05). No significant association was found between disclosure status and ART adherence, and no significant predictors of ART adherence were identified in the adjusted model.
Conclusion: ART adherence was not significantly associated with HIV disclosure status. Strengthening adolescent-friendly psychosocial support, caregiver engagement, and stigma-reduction interventions may improve treatment outcomes among ALHIV.

WACP/26/003  Awareness and participation in community-led monitoring among people living with HIV and key populations
Authors: Temitope I, Ilori T, Agbo F, Aguolu R, Chinonso A, Egwu JE, Abraham A, Uchendu O
Affiliations: National Agency for the Control of AIDS (NACA), Abuja; University of Ibadan; University College Hospital, Ibadan
Correspondence: obioma234@gmail.com
Background: Community-led monitoring (CLM) is a structured mechanism that promotes accountability, service quality, and inclusiveness within health systems. Evidence on the extent of meaningful engagement of People Living with HIV (PLHIV) and Key Populations (KP) in CLM activities remains limited.
Aim: This study assessed PLHIV awareness and explored the roles of PLHIV, KP, and other stakeholders in CLM activities.
Methods: This cross-sectional study utilised mixed methods to study participants across 10 states in Nigeria. Multistage probability sampling was used to select 1,075 participants for the quantitative component, while 50 participants were purposively recruited for qualitative interviews. Data were obtained using structured questionnaires, key informant interviews, and in-depth interviews. Analysis was done using IBM SPSS version 26 and NVivo 15. Ethical approval was obtained before data collection.
Results: Majority of the respondents were females (71.5%) and PLHIV (85.1%). Half (54.7%) were aware of CLM initiatives, with 74.5% of these actively participating in CLM activities. Frequently reported roles were educational (75.3%), data collection (65.7%), and advocacy (52.3%) roles. Fewer respondents provided feedback to service providers (48.5%) or monitored implementation of recommended changes (24.0%). Qualitative findings highlighted contributions of PLHIV and KP members in community engagement, evidence generation, and advocacy for improved service access. Stakeholders described their roles in advocacy, policy guidance, technical support, mentorship, and community mobilisation.
Conclusion: Participation in CLM is encouraging, but gaps exist in feedback mechanisms and implementation monitoring. Strengthening structured feedback systems and inclusive engagement strategies is essential to improve the quality and impact of CLM in Nigeria.

WACP/26/004  Community-led monitoring and barriers to HIV service access in Nigeria
Authors: Uchendu O, Ilori T, Agbo F, Aguolu R, Chinonso A, Egwu JE, Abraham A
Affiliations: University of Ibadan; University College Hospital, Ibadan; National Agency for the Control of AIDS (NACA), Abuja
Correspondence: obioma234@gmail.com
Background: Despite advances in HIV treatment and expanded access to antiretroviral therapy (ART), barriers such as stigma and service delivery gaps limit equitable access to HIV services. Community-led monitoring (CLM) is a structured approach to identifying barriers and improving program implementation in HIV service delivery.
Aim: This study explored perceptions of People Living with HIV (PLHIV), key populations (KPs), and stakeholders on CLM's role in service access barriers.
Methods: The study, conducted across 10 Nigerian states, utilised a descriptive cross-sectional mixed-methods design. Quantitative data were collected from 1,075 participants using multistage probability sampling, while 20 in-depth interviews and 30 key informant interviews were conducted using purposive sampling. Data were analysed using IBM SPSS version 26 (quantitative) and NVivo 15 (qualitative). Ethical approval and consent were obtained before data collection.
Results: Majority were aged ≥ 40 years (55.2%). HIV testing (89.9%) and ART services (87.6%) were the most available, whereas PMTCT (55.4%) and harm reduction services (14.2%) were less available. Distance and transportation challenges (76.1%) and stigma and discrimination (55.5%) were frequently reported barriers. Respondents reported that CLM improved access to HIV testing (93.0%), ART (91.2%), and PMTCT (68.7%) services. Qualitative findings indicated that CLM facilitated free HIV services, community ART delivery, and partnerships with local pharmacies. CLM implementation reduced stock-outs and improved client-provider relationships.
Conclusion: CLM improved HIV service access by dismantling key structural, financial, and social barriers. Scaling community ART delivery, strengthening local partnerships, and stigma reduction should be prioritised to strengthen equitable access and continuity of HIV care.

WACP/26/005  Cost-Effectiveness and Implementation Model of All-Oral Short-Course Regimens for Multidrug-Resistant Tuberculosis: A Systematic Review with Policy and Geospatial Implications for Nigeria
Authors: Christie AC, Whitley K, Udayan U, Idowu EO, Rafee AR, Wainwright B, Le E, Tanwar A, Madavan S, Grimadeau C, Loyd K, Phillips C, Castellarin K
Affiliations: New York University; Trinity School of Medicine; University of Texas Rio Grande Valley; University of Ibadan / Harvard Medical Faculty Physicians; University of Veterinary and Animal Sciences, Lahore; American University of Antigua; American University of the Caribbean; University of Minnesota
Correspondence: dr.idowuemmanuel@gmail.com
Background: Multidrug-resistant tuberculosis (MDR-TB) constitutes one of the biggest problems in the global fight against infectious diseases, as evidenced by the poor success rate (about 52%) of the standard injectables-based regimen used for treating the condition. The cost and logistical implications associated with MDR-TB treatment are prohibitive for most patients. In Nigeria, which is responsible for 4.8% of the worldwide TB disease burden and reported MDR-TB prevalence rates of 2.9% and 14.3% among new and retreatment cases respectively, the problem is further exacerbated by unique structural factors making the use of the traditional 18–20-month regimen unsuitable for most patients. The aim was to conduct a systematic review of the scientific literature on the efficacy, safety, cost-efficiency, and programmatic scalability of shorter all-oral MDR-TB regimens, including BPaL, BPaLM, STREAM Stage 2, NExT, and MDR-END, and to design an evidence-based geospatial policy for their implementation in Nigeria.
Methods: Following PRISMA guidelines, a systematic literature search was carried out in six databases (PubMed, EMBASE, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, WHO IRIS, and NTBLCP grey literature repositories). A total of 30 papers published between 2017 and 2025 were selected for inclusion based on predefined eligibility criteria. A geospatial analysis of the spatial distribution of Nigeria's MDR-TB burden, facility access, and population density was done to determine the programmatic feasibility of decentralising MDR-TB treatment.
Results: The short-course all-oral regimens proved highly successful, exhibiting 82–90% treatment success rates compared to conventional treatments' 52.3%. The NExT study had a 90% culture conversion, while BPaL drugs showed 89% positive results in the ZeNix and TB-PRACTECAL studies. Geographical distribution analysis revealed that the Very High burden MDR-TB regions of Nigeria — namely the North-West and North-East geopolitical zones — have inadequate access to MDR-TB facilities located over 50 km from patients' residences. Economically, the adoption of shorter regimens resulted in significant savings per patient (cost saving of 70.5% from $9,500 to $2,800).
Conclusion: These findings support the consideration of implementing shorter all-oral MDR-TB regimens in Nigeria, alongside community-oriented decentralisation strategies to improve treatment access and outcomes.

WACP/26/006  Epidemic preparedness among healthcare workers in Nigerian healthcare facilities
Authors: Eseigbe P, Mbah IO, Amaku CU, Chima GAA, Bello CSS
Affiliations: Department of Family Medicine; Department of Internal Medicine; Department of Medical Microbiology – Bingham University / Bingham University Teaching Hospital, Jos, Nigeria
Correspondence: trishdaniels09@gmail.com
Background: Recently, epidemics of emerging and re-emerging infectious diseases have occurred with increasing frequency, including severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS), the novel coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19), Ebola virus disease, Lassa fever, and the re-emerged Monkeypox.
Aim: This study sought to explore the knowledge and perceptions of preparedness among healthcare workers at Bingham University Teaching Hospital, Jos, regarding an outbreak or epidemic, to optimise the management of epidemics.
Methods: This was a cross-sectional study. A modified self-administered international epidemic survey tool on preparedness across five scales — clinical, environmental, communication, human resources, and general preparedness — was administered. Data were analysed using SPSS version 24. The level of significance was set at p < 0.05.
Results: A total of 381 healthcare workers completed the survey. The mean age of the participants was 35.3 ± 11.24 years. Thirty-three (8.7%) were doctors, 99 (26.0%) were nurses, and the others were pharmacists, laboratory scientists, and administrative staff. In general preparedness, 238 (62.5%) of the participants perceived that the healthcare facility was well prepared, and 231 (60.6%) agreed that they were personally prepared to respond to an epidemic. The participants' age (χ² = 24.370, p < 0.0001), educational level (χ² = 22.076, p < 0.0001), and the number of years at work (χ² = 18.781, p = 0.001) were significantly associated with their personal preparedness. Their area of discipline was significantly related to their perception of the facility's preparedness (χ² = 16.364, p = 0.003) and their personal preparedness (χ² = 9.531, p = 0.048) for an epidemic.
Conclusion: This study demonstrated a sub-optimal level of preparedness for an epidemic. There is a need for continued regular review of preparedness strategies in healthcare facilities to maintain readiness.

WACP/26/007  Antibiotic exposure, WHO AWARe classification and nasal MRSA colonisation in a tertiary hospital
Authors: Okunbor HN, Otaigbe II, Oluwole TO, Elikwu CJ
Affiliations: Department of Medical Microbiology, Babcock University Teaching Hospital, Nigeria
Correspondence: okunborhilary@gmail.com
Background: Antimicrobial stewardship is essential in reducing inappropriate antibiotic use and limiting selective pressure that promotes methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) colonisation. Nasal MRSA carriage serves as an important reservoir for transmission and reflects prior antibiotic exposure patterns.
Objective: To assess the association between antibiotic exposure and nasal MRSA colonisation using the WHO AWARe classification, antibiotic class effects, dose-response relationships, and combination therapy.
Methods: A cross-sectional study involving 400 participants admitted to Babcock University Teaching Hospital was conducted. Nasal swabs were screened for MRSA, and previous antibiotic exposure was classified according to WHO AWARe categories and antibiotic classes. Associations were analysed using odds ratios (OR), 95% confidence intervals (CI), chi-square tests, and trend analysis.
Results: Antibiotic exposure was significantly associated with MRSA colonisation (OR = 8.57; 95% CI: 2.01–36.50; p < 0.001). Watch antibiotics showed a strong association (OR = 8.99; 95% CI: 2.12–38.14; p < 0.001), whereas Access antibiotics showed no significant association (OR = 0.99; p = 0.983). Fluoroquinolones (OR = 6.74) and cephalosporins (OR = 5.85) were major risk factors. MRSA prevalence increased from 1.6% among unexposed participants to 42.9% among those exposed to multiple antibiotic classes (χ² = 28.45; p < 0.0001). Combination therapy involving fluoroquinolones and cephalosporins showed the highest risk (OR = 50.83; 95% CI: 10.26–251.90).
Conclusion: High-risk antibiotics, particularly Watch-category agents, significantly increase nasal MRSA colonisation and highlight major antimicrobial stewardship gaps.

WACP/26/008  Previous antibiotic exposure patterns and nasal MRSA colonisation in a tertiary hospital
Authors: Okunbor HN, Oluwole TO, Otaigbe II, Elikwu CJ
Affiliations: Department of Medical Microbiology and Parasitology, Babcock University Teaching Hospital, Nigeria
Correspondence: okunborhilary@gmail.com
Background: Exposure to antimicrobial agents is a major driver of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) and may influence colonisation by Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA). Nasal MRSA carriage serves as an important reservoir for transmission and subsequent invasive infections, especially in healthcare settings. The study evaluated the association between nasal MRSA colonisation and previous antibiotic exposure patterns, with antimicrobial stewardship (AMS) implications.
Methods: This was a cross-sectional study of inpatients in a tertiary hospital in South-west Nigeria. Data on prior antibiotic exposure were extracted from case notes. Antibiotics were categorised into Access, Watch, and Reserve categories based on the WHO AWaRe classification. Nasal swabs were collected and screened for MRSA using standard microbiological methods. Descriptive and inferential statistics were conducted.
Results: Nasal MRSA colonisation rate was 9% (36/400), with prior antibiotic exposure in 94.4% (34/36). Antibiotic exposure was significantly associated with MRSA colonisation (OR = 8.57; p < 0.001), with prevalence increasing from 1.6% among unexposed individuals to 42.9% among patients exposed to multiple antibiotic classes (χ² = 28.45; p < 0.0001). Exposure to WHO Watch antibiotics showed a strong association with MRSA colonisation (OR = 8.99; p < 0.001). At the class level, fluoroquinolones (OR = 6.74; p < 0.001) and cephalosporins (OR = 5.85; p < 0.001) were the strongest predictors. Combined exposure to fluoroquinolones and cephalosporins conferred the highest risk (OR = 50.83; 95% CI: 10.26–251.90).
Conclusion: These findings reinforce the urgent need for targeted antimicrobial stewardship interventions aimed at optimising antibiotic prescribing and reducing selective pressure which drives AMR.

WACP/26/009  Comparative performance of sepsis prognostic scores in public and private hospitals in Nigeria
Authors: Akase IE, Emoruwa E, Asefon TI, Mfon KEO, Nwanma CE, Emekekwue CO, Ogunyanwo MA, Aghulor GO, Imoukhuede LE, Sanusi SO, Mfon EEM, Aitanu RA, Oghide O
Affiliations: Department of Medicine, College of Medicine, University of Lagos; Infectious Diseases Unit, Lagos University Teaching Hospital; Iwosan Lagoon Hospital; Havana Specialist Hospital; Lagos State Primary Healthcare Board
Correspondence: iorhenakase@yahoo.com
Background: Sepsis remains a leading cause of mortality in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), yet the comparative performance of prognostic scoring systems in these settings — particularly across public and private hospitals — remains poorly characterised. This study compared the discriminative ability of qSOFA, SIRS, mSOFA, and NEWS for predicting in-hospital mortality, ICU requirement, and septic shock in adult sepsis patients in Lagos, Nigeria.
Methods: This retrospective comparative observational cohort study included adults with sepsis admitted to one public and one private tertiary hospital in Lagos between 1 January 2023 and 31 December 2024. Prognostic scores were calculated using data from the first 24 hours. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was performed for each score against three outcomes. Area under the curve (AUC) with 95% confidence intervals was estimated using nonparametric methods.
Results: Among 431 patients (401 with complete data for ROC analyses), mSOFA demonstrated the highest discriminative performance for all outcomes: in-hospital mortality (AUC 0.884, 95% CI 0.848–0.921), ICU requirement (AUC 0.863, 95% CI 0.822–0.904), and septic shock (AUC 0.898, 95% CI 0.862–0.934). NEWS showed moderate-to-good performance (AUC range 0.712–0.815), qSOFA fair discrimination (AUC 0.661–0.726), and SIRS the lowest accuracy (AUC 0.613–0.658).
Conclusion: In this LMIC cohort spanning public and private hospitals, mSOFA consistently outperformed qSOFA, SIRS, and NEWS for predicting mortality, ICU requirement, and septic shock. mSOFA is feasible in resource-limited settings and should be considered for routine sepsis risk stratification.

WACP/26/011  A digital referral and decision-support model linking primary health care and tertiary hospitals
Authors: Oludele HD
Affiliations: Medical School College of Medicine, University of Ibadan
Correspondence: magbimlucy@gmail.com
Background: Poor integration between primary healthcare centres (PHCs) and tertiary hospitals often causes unnecessary referrals, overcrowded specialist facilities, and delayed management of critical patients. Limited access to rapid specialist input at local clinics contributes to inefficient patient transfer and suboptimal early intervention.
Objective: To propose a lightweight, interoperable Electronic Medical Record (EMR) system with AI-assisted voice clerking and real-time teleconsultation to improve referral efficiency and optimise primary-to-tertiary care transitions.
Methodology: The framework uses a low-bandwidth EMR at local clinics synchronised with a centralised tertiary hub. An AI-powered voice-to-text module enables rapid hands-free history taking, automated documentation, and triage. For complex cases, clinicians can activate a secure specialist consultation feature, allowing tertiary physicians to access synchronised records, review transcribed notes, and provide immediate remote guidance. Non-critical patients can be managed locally, while critically ill patients receive specialist-directed stabilisation before transfer.
Expected Impact: The system is designed to reduce unnecessary specialist referrals, decongest tertiary hospitals, and improve allocation of specialised resources. Early specialist-guided stabilisation may reduce diagnostic delays, morbidity, and mortality in emergency referrals.
Conclusion: A voice-optimised, synchronised EMR can strengthen communication between local clinics and tertiary hospitals, improve triage accuracy, and enhance healthcare efficiency in resource-limited settings.

WACP/26/012  Acoustic biomarkers and telemedicine for neurodegenerative disorders and respiratory infection surveillance
Authors: Oludele HD
Affiliations: 600-level College of Medicine, University of Ibadan
Correspondence: magbimlucy@gmail.com
Background: Delayed diagnosis of neurodegenerative disorders often limits opportunities for early intervention because detection relies on late-stage clinical symptoms. Likewise, surveillance of airborne acute respiratory infections (ARIs) is hindered by delays in hospital presentation. Early vocal biomarkers — including phonatory instability in Parkinson's disease and altered speech pauses in Alzheimer's disease — provide an opportunity for earlier detection through passive acoustic monitoring.
Objective: To propose a tele-acoustic screening framework capable of detecting early neurodegenerative decline in elderly populations while simultaneously monitoring community-level respiratory symptom trends.
Methodology / Clinical Integration: The system passively analyses speech patterns during routine phone calls using on-device acoustic processing, without transmitting raw audio. Longitudinal changes in speech articulation and phonation are monitored for deviations associated with Parkinsonian hypophonia or Alzheimer's-related semantic decline. Significant deviations trigger prompts for geriatric assessment. Concurrently, the platform quantifies respiratory sounds such as coughing. Aggregated anonymised geo-tagged data can help public health agencies identify ARI clusters and predict outbreaks before increases in hospital admissions.
Expected Impact: Clinically, the system may support earlier neurological evaluation and intervention. Epidemiologically, it offers a scalable, low-cost syndromic surveillance tool for infectious disease monitoring.
Conclusion: Integrating acoustic biomarker analysis into existing telecommunication infrastructure could strengthen geriatric care and public health surveillance in resource-limited settings, particularly in West Africa.
